My Family • My Home

The people who shaped me, loved me, and still do — every single day

I want to share a piece of my story, continuing from where I left off in my previous reflections. I have a little brother named Marco Benjamin Ricardos Galinato, and an older sister named Abigail Grace Galinato. I used to feel a bit envious of their names—so distinct, elegant, and unique—while mine felt rather simple in comparison. But over time, I’ve come to cherish my own name. It may not be as elaborate, but it holds meaning, identity, and warmth for me. It is mine, and for that, I am grateful.

Growing up in the province, I was raised away from them for a time. When my mother brought me back, I remember feeling shy, almost hesitant, to connect with my siblings. Everything was unfamiliar. But as time passed, the walls slowly came down. Like most children, we argued, fought over the smallest things, and shouted at each other more than we probably should have. But beneath those quarrels was something deeper—a bond that time could never erase.

There's no such thing as a perfect family, I’ve learned, but there is such a thing as a better one: a family that grows, forgives, and loves despite the flaws.

My Mother — Merlie Galinato

She is the very heart of our home: caring, patient, strong, and deeply hardworking. She’s also an amazing cook. I especially remember her spaghetti — the dish that defined every celebration in our household. She used to be quite strict when I was younger, something I misunderstood at the time. But now, as a young man of 17 (soon to turn 18), I understand more clearly. I see her not just as a mother, but as a woman who has given everything for her children. She’s been working since she was just around my age, perhaps 18 or so, taking jobs as a caretaker and a maid to support the family. It was never easy, but she carried every burden with grace.

My Father — Benjamin Galinato

He is a man of few words but immense strength. He has always struck me as fierce, stern when needed, and unyielding when it came to discipline. But behind that exterior is a kind and noble man. He works as a truck driver for a private company, often delivering high-value car parts, especially carbon fiber and other premium components. At home, he’s our resident handyman—skilled in basic carpentry and electrical work. Truly, a well-rounded man who builds, fixes, and provides.

Looking back, I now understand things I couldn't before. The rules, the discipline, the sacrifices—they weren’t meant to burden me. They were acts of love, shown not always in words, but in long days of work, in meals cooked with care, in lessons taught through silence and action.

This is my family. Imperfect, loud, loving, real, and better every day.
